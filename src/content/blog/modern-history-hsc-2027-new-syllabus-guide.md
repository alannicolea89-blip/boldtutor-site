If you are starting Year 11 or 12 Modern History soon, or already in the thick of it, the 2027 syllabus brings some meaningful changes worth understanding early. The structure of the course stays broadly familiar — four components, each requiring significant depth — but the specific content, particularly in the Core Study, has shifted.

This post breaks down each of the four components in plain language, with dot points to make the structure easy to follow. It is designed to give you a working map of the course before you go deep on any single area.

---

## Component 1: Core Study — Democracy and Dictatorship 1919–1939

This is the compulsory component that every Modern History student in NSW studies, regardless of their other choices. The 2027 syllabus focuses the Core Study on the period between the end of World War One and the beginning of World War Two — a time when democracy was under serious pressure across the world.

### What the Core Study is asking you to think about

- Why did democratic governments collapse in so many countries after 1919?
- What conditions made it possible for dictators to gain and hold power?
- What did life actually look like for people living under authoritarian regimes?
- What threats did these regimes pose to peace internationally?

Germany and the Nazi regime are the main case study, but the syllabus also requires you to examine two other dictatorships from the interwar period. This means you will need a solid understanding of context beyond Germany — Italy under Mussolini, Stalin's Soviet Union, Franco's Spain, and others are all relevant depending on what your teacher selects.

### The Paris Peace Conference and its consequences

This is the starting point of the Core Study. The 1919 Paris Peace Conference ended World War One but created the conditions for enormous instability:

- The Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh terms on Germany, including the war guilt clause, reparations, and significant territorial losses
- Germany was humiliated rather than integrated into a stable postwar order
- New nations were created in Central and Eastern Europe, many of them fragile and vulnerable to internal conflict
- The League of Nations was established but lacked real enforcement power, particularly without United States membership
- Economic instability across Europe, worsened by the Great Depression from 1929, made populations desperate and ready to listen to radical alternatives

### Interwar dictatorships

The syllabus requires you to understand the general conditions that allowed dictators to rise, and then examine two specific dictatorships in depth:

- Conditions that enabled dictators included: economic crisis, unemployment, humiliation following WWI, fear of communism, weak democratic institutions, nationalist resentment, and the appeal of strong charismatic leadership
- Common features of interwar dictatorships include: single-party rule, suppression of political opposition, control of the media, use of propaganda, state terror and secret police, cult of personality around the leader, and aggressive nationalism
- Italy under Mussolini (from 1922) and Stalin's Soviet Union are among the most commonly studied, though Japan, Spain and Hungary are also options

### The collapse of German democracy

This section focuses on the Weimar Republic — the democratic government that ruled Germany from 1919 to 1933 — and why it failed:

- The Weimar Republic was born in defeat and associated with the humiliating peace settlement from the start
- It faced serious political challenges from both the left (communist uprisings) and the right (nationalist and paramilitary groups including the early Nazi Party)
- Economic crises hit hard: hyperinflation in 1923, then mass unemployment following the 1929 Great Depression
- Political parties were fragmented and unable to form stable governing coalitions
- The constitution itself had weaknesses — Article 48 allowed the President to rule by emergency decree, which was used increasingly as parliamentary government broke down
- Hitler was appointed Chancellor in January 1933 through legal means, exploiting political miscalculation by conservative politicians who thought they could control him
- Within months, the democratic system had been effectively dismantled

### Source analysis: the Nazi regime in power 1933–1939

This is a distinct part of the Core Study that requires you to work with primary sources — not just write essays about content, but analyse and interpret evidence. The key areas covered are:

- The initial consolidation of power 1933–1934: the Reichstag Fire, the Enabling Act, the Night of the Long Knives, and Hitler becoming Führer after Hindenburg's death
- Nazi ideology: racial hierarchy, antisemitism, extreme nationalism, Social Darwinism, the concept of *Lebensraum* (living space), and the *Volksgemeinschaft* (people's community)
- The impact of Nazi ideology and policies on different groups:
  - Jewish people: escalating persecution from legal discrimination (Nuremberg Laws 1935) to the violence of Kristallnacht (1938)
  - Women: pushed toward a domestic role as wives and mothers; the three Ks — *Kinder, Küche, Kirche* (children, kitchen, church)
  - Youth: the Hitler Youth and League of German Girls shaped young people's values and created loyalty to the regime
  - Workers: the German Labour Front replaced trade unions; workers gained job security but lost rights
  - Other minorities: disabled people, Roma, LGBTQ+ individuals, and political opponents all faced persecution
- Methods of control:
  - Laws: Enabling Act, Nuremberg Laws, Decree of the Reich President for the Protection of People and State
  - Censorship: control of the press, book burnings, regulation of art and culture
  - Propaganda: Goebbels and the Ministry of Public Enlightenment shaped every form of media
  - Repression and terror: the SS, Gestapo, concentration camps, and the systematic elimination of opposition
- Opposition to the Nazi regime: the White Rose student resistance, church opposition (including Pastor Niemöller), communist and socialist underground networks, and the July 1944 assassination attempt on Hitler

## Component 2: National Study

In this component you study the history of one country across a specific period of the 20th century. Your teacher will choose the country, but here is a quick summary of each option so you know what you might be dealing with.

The eight options are:

- **Australia 1918–1949**: covers the interwar years, the Great Depression, and Australia's experience in World War Two, including the threat of invasion and the social changes that followed
- **China 1927–1949**: the Chinese Civil War between the Nationalists and the Communists, the Japanese invasion, and Mao Zedong's eventual victory in 1949
- **Cuba 1940–1991**: from the pre-revolutionary period through Batista's dictatorship, Castro's revolution in 1959, the Cold War confrontations (including the Cuban Missile Crisis), and the decades of communist rule
- **Indonesia 1945–2004**: from independence from the Dutch, through Sukarno's leadership, the violent transition to Suharto's New Order, and the eventual return to democracy
- **Iran 1945–1989**: Cold War politics, the oil nationalisation crisis, the Shah's rule, and the 1979 Islamic Revolution under Khomeini
- **Japan 1904–1937**: the rise of Japanese militarism, imperial expansion in Asia and the Pacific, and the path toward the Second World War
- **Russia and the Soviet Union 1917–1941**: the Russian Revolution, the civil war, Lenin's leadership, Stalin's rise to power, collectivisation, and the purges
- **United States 1919–1941**: the Red Scare, the Jazz Age and Prohibition, the Great Depression, Roosevelt's New Deal, and the period leading to American involvement in World War Two

For each of these, you are expected to understand key individuals, significant events, turning points, and how political, social and economic factors interacted across the period.

## Component 3: Peace and Conflict

Here you study one of six major international conflicts or periods of tension. Again, your teacher or school will make the selection. The options are:

- **Conflict in Europe 1935–1945**: from the remilitarisation of the Rhineland and the policy of appeasement through to the end of World War Two in Europe, including key turning points like the Battle of Britain, Operation Barbarossa, and D-Day
- **Conflict in the Pacific 1937–1951**: Japan's expansion from 1937, the attack on Pearl Harbor, the island-hopping campaign, the use of the atomic bombs, and the aftermath including the Korean War
- **The Cold War 1945–1991**: the ideological and geopolitical contest between the United States and the Soviet Union from the end of WWII to the collapse of the Soviet system, including key crises, proxy wars, and the arms race
- **The Arab–Israeli conflict 1948–2000**: from the establishment of the state of Israel through multiple wars, the PLO, the peace process, and the ongoing question of Palestinian statehood
- **Conflict in Vietnam, Cambodia and Laos 1954–1976**: the First and Second Indochina Wars, American involvement, the Ho Chi Minh Trail, the fall of Saigon, and the broader regional consequences including the Khmer Rouge
- **Conflict in the Gulf 1980–2017**: the Iran–Iraq War, the first Gulf War, the 2003 invasion of Iraq, and the rise of ISIS

For each option you are expected to understand:
- The causes and origins of the conflict
- Key events, turning points and campaigns
- The roles of significant individuals, nations and organisations
- The consequences and legacy of the conflict

## Component 4: Change in the Modern World

This component focuses on political and social change over a defined period, with particular attention to the role of individuals and groups in driving that change. The six options are:

- **The Nuclear Age 1945–2021**: the development of nuclear weapons, the arms race, nuclear testing, nonproliferation efforts, and the ongoing threat of nuclear conflict into the 21st century
- **Civil rights in the United States 1945–1972**: the African American civil rights movement from the postwar years through the Montgomery Bus Boycott, the sit-ins, the March on Washington, key legislation, and the work of figures including Rosa Parks, Martin Luther King Jr., and Malcolm X
- **The Anti-Apartheid Movement in South Africa 1948–1994**: the introduction of apartheid, the ANC's resistance, the role of Nelson Mandela and figures like Steve Biko, international sanctions, and the transition to democracy
- **The struggle for democracy in Burma 1948–2021**: independence from Britain, military coups, the 1988 uprising, Aung San Suu Kyi and the National League for Democracy, and the 2021 military takeover
- **From the Cultural Revolution to Tiananmen Square 1966–1989**: Mao's Cultural Revolution, the Gang of Four, Deng Xiaoping's reforms, and the 1989 pro-democracy movement and its brutal suppression
- **The changing world order 1989–2016**: the end of the Cold War, the collapse of the Soviet Union, the rise of China, the War on Terror, and shifting patterns of global power in the post-Cold War era

These topics reward students who can think about change over time — what drove the change, who resisted it, what strategies succeeded, and what the long-term consequences were.

## A few things worth knowing before you start

- **Source analysis is built into the Core Study** in a way that is more structured than in previous syllabuses. You will need to practise working with primary sources — photographs, speeches, propaganda posters, government documents, and eyewitness accounts — not just read about the period.
- **Depth matters more than breadth**. You are not expected to know everything about a 20-year period. You are expected to know key developments, turning points, and significant individuals in genuine depth.
- **The essay is still central**. Modern History rewards clear argument, well-selected evidence, and consistent use of historical concepts like causation, continuity and change, perspective, and significance.
- **The Core Study connects to everything else**. The interwar period you study in Component 1 often overlaps with the conflicts and national studies in Components 2, 3 and 4. Students who make those connections tend to write stronger responses.
- **Start with the content your teacher has chosen** for Components 2, 3 and 4. Each option has a large body of potential content. Getting clarity on what is in scope early will make revision much more manageable.
