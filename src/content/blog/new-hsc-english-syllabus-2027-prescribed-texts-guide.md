In 2024, NESA released the new English Advanced and English Standard syllabuses, along with a fresh set of prescribed texts for the HSC. These texts will be examined from 2027 and the prescriptions have been extended to run through to 2030, giving students and teachers a longer runway with the same list.

If you are in Year 10 or starting Year 11 now, these are the texts you will be studying. This post is an attempt to give you a useful first look at what each text is about, what makes it interesting, and why it might have been chosen. The information here comes from a range of literary reviews, publisher notes, and critical commentary available online. It is a starting point, not a definitive guide.

## English Advanced and Standard: Texts and Human Experiences

This is the shared module between Advanced and Standard. Both courses study texts in this module, though the approach differs slightly between them.

### Prose Fiction

**Jessica Au, *Cold Enough for Snow***

A slim, spare novella from a Melbourne writer. The story is simple on the surface: a young woman travels to Japan with her mother. They visit galleries, eat in cafés, walk through autumn streets. But beneath that quiet surface, the novella explores something much more difficult to name.

- The relationship between the narrator and her mother is careful, almost formal. Warmth and distance exist side by side throughout the book.
- The novella moves between the present trip and fragments of the narrator's memory, raising questions about how clearly we ever understand our parents.
- Au's prose style is notably restrained. There is space between sentences. What goes unsaid is as important as what is said.
- Themes of diaspora run through the book quietly. The mother grew up in Hong Kong before immigrating; the narrator is second-generation. The Japan trip is, among other things, a way of finding neutral ground.

It is a gentle but unsettling book. Students who enjoy introspective, quiet fiction will find it rewarding.

**David Malouf, *An Imaginary Life***

Malouf's 1978 novel is a fictional meditation on the Roman poet Ovid, who was exiled by Emperor Augustus to a remote village on the edge of the empire. It is one of the great Australian novels and a standard of the HSC for good reason.

- Ovid, a poet of extraordinary sophistication, finds himself in a place with no Latin, no writing, and no culture he recognises.
- The novel becomes an exploration of language itself: what happens when a person who defines themselves through words loses access to words?
- A feral child who lives in the wild becomes central to the second half of the novel. The relationship between Ovid and the Child raises questions about civilisation, nature, and what it means to be human.
- Themes include: exile, transformation (Ovid's own *Metamorphoses* echo throughout), the limits of language, and the connection between self and place.

**Henry Lawson, *The Penguin Henry Lawson Short Stories***

The prescribed stories are: 'The Drover's Wife', 'The Union Buries Its Dead', 'Shooting the Moon', 'Our Pipes', 'The Loaded Dog'.

Lawson is one of the foundational voices of Australian literature, and these stories have been studied in various forms for generations. They hold up.

- 'The Drover's Wife' is probably his most famous story: a woman alone on a remote property deals with a snake under the floorboards while her husband is away droving. It is deceptively simple and has been reinterpreted many times.
- 'The Union Buries Its Dead' is a darkly comic account of a funeral for a stranger in outback New South Wales. It is as much about community and class as it is about death.
- 'The Loaded Dog' is lighter: a story of larrikin bushmen and a dog who does not understand he is carrying a lit explosive.
- Themes across the collection include: mateship, class, hardship, isolation, the bush as both threatening and defining, and an understated emotional life that often says more in what it leaves out.

### Poetry

**Rosemary Dobson, Selected Poems**

The prescribed poems are: 'Young Girl at a Window', 'Summer's End', 'Cock Crow', 'A Fine Thing', 'Child of Our Time', 'Piltdown Man', 'Every Man His Own Sculptor'.

Dobson (1920–2012) was one of Australia's most respected poets and one of the most underread outside academic circles. Her work is precise, classical in structure, and deeply attentive to art and memory.

- Many of her poems engage directly with visual art, particularly painting, as a way of thinking about perception and time.
- She is interested in what art preserves and what it cannot preserve: what gets frozen and what keeps changing.
- 'Child of Our Time' confronts the violence of the twentieth century with controlled grief.
- Her style is formal without being stiff. Students who find poetry difficult often respond well to Dobson because her poems are structurally clear and her images are concrete.

**Gwen Harwood, Selected Poems**

The prescribed poems are: 'The Glass Jar', 'The Violets', 'At Mornington', 'Father and Child, I and II', 'A Valediction', 'Beyond Metaphor', 'The Sharpness of Death'.

Harwood is already well known to many HSC students from previous prescriptions. She is one of Australia's finest poets and these poems represent some of her best work.

- 'Father and Child' is a two-part poem about a daughter's relationship with her father across time. It moves from a childhood act of violence against a bird to the father's death. It is devastating and precise.
- 'The Violets' deals with childhood memory, the smell of flowers, and the way the past reaches into the present without warning.
- 'At Mornington' is about swimming and mortality and the way the body carries experience across time.
- Harwood's central concerns include: time, mortality, music, parenthood, the intellectual life of women, and the experience of grief. She writes with great emotional intelligence and formal control.

**Samuel Wagan Watson, *Love Poems and Death Threats***

The prescribed poems are: 'The Remedy of Butterflies', 'Finn', 'Let's Talk!', 'El Diablo Highway', 'Blacktracker... Blackwriter... Blacksubject', 'End of Days', 'Addendum'.

Watson is a Birri Gubba/Mununjali poet from Queensland and one of the most distinctive voices in contemporary Australian poetry. This collection has received significant critical attention and his inclusion on the new prescription is a welcome addition.

- The collection is described as mapping the songlines of an Australia scarred by invasion and injustice, but it is also funny, urban, and alive with pop culture references and vernacular energy.
- The love poems are raw and honest. The political poems are precise and uncompromising.
- Watson is deeply interested in the relationship between Indigenous experience and place, and between personal grief and collective history.
- His style mixes registers: formal and colloquial, lyrical and satirical, tender and confrontational. Students who find canonical poetry difficult often connect strongly with Watson's voice.

### Drama, Film and Nonfiction

**Richard Flanagan, *Question 7* (nonfiction)**

Flanagan is one of Australia's most celebrated novelists (*The Narrow Road to the Deep North*), and *Question 7* is a work of literary nonfiction published in 2023. It is part memoir, part meditation on history, science, and the randomness of existence.

- The title references a Gestapo interrogation form on which Question 7 asks for the subject's race. The book examines how history turns on contingencies.
- Flanagan reflects on his father's survival as a prisoner of war, his own childhood in Tasmania, and the way literature and writing offer a way of making meaning from chaos.
- Themes include: fate, storytelling, the relationship between history and individual life, Tasmania, and the question of what writing is for.

**Michael Gow, *Away* (drama)**

*Away* (1986) is one of the most studied plays in Australian drama and an HSC staple for many years in other prescriptions. Its return here is well earned.

- Three families go on holiday in the summer of 1967/68, a period of significant cultural change in Australia.
- One of the characters, Tom, is terminally ill. His situation shapes the emotional landscape of the play without defining it.
- The play draws on Shakespeare's *A Midsummer Night's Dream* and *King Lear* in ways worth exploring.
- Themes include: mortality, healing, class tension in Australian society, the transformative power of landscape, and reconciliation within families.

**Rachel Perkins, *One Night the Moon* (film)**

A 2001 Australian musical film set in outback New South Wales in the 1930s. Based loosely on real events.

- A young girl wanders into the bush and goes missing. Her father refuses to let an Aboriginal tracker help because of his racial prejudice, with tragic consequences.
- The film uses sparse, haunting music to carry much of its emotional weight.
- Themes include: racism, grief, the land, and the cost of refusing to listen to Indigenous knowledge and people.

## English Standard: Language, Identity and Culture

This module is specific to English Standard.

**Jhumpa Lahiri, *The Namesake*** deals with identity across generations of an Indian American family. **Tara June Winch, *Swallow the Air*** is a powerful Australian novel about a young Aboriginal woman's search for identity and country. **Raymond Antrobus, *The Perseverance*** is a landmark debut collection exploring Deaf identity, mixed-race identity, and the complex relationship with language when language itself is partly inaccessible to you. **Adam Aitken, Kim Cheng Boey and Michelle Cahill's *Contemporary Asian Australian Poets*** is an anthology representing a range of voices writing from and about Asian Australian experience.

Film includes **Colm Bairéad's *The Quiet Girl*** (2022), a tender Irish film about a quiet child sent to stay with relatives for the summer who finds, possibly for the first time, that she is truly seen; and drama includes **Alana Valentine's *Shafana and Aunt Sarrinah***, which centres on a conversation between two Muslim Australian women about the decision to wear the hijab.

## English Standard: Close Study of Literature

**MT Anderson, *Feed*** (2002) is a prescient YA dystopian novel set in a future where the internet is directly connected to the human brain. It is sharp, satirical, and uncomfortably relevant to discussions of social media, attention, and corporate power.

**Robbie Arnott, *Limberlost*** (2022) is a quiet, lyrical Australian novel set across decades in a Tasmanian river valley. A young man spends a summer hunting rabbits while his brothers are at war. The novel follows him across his life, exploring masculinity, grief, family, and the relationship between humans and the natural world.

**Carol Ann Duffy's poetry** (including 'War Photographer', 'Stealing', 'Little Red Cap', 'Mrs Midas') is known for its dramatic monologues and its habit of giving voice to figures on the margins or in the shadows of familiar stories. **Oodgeroo Noonuccal's *My People*** brings one of Australia's first published Aboriginal poets into the close study framework, with poems that range from political protest to celebration of culture and country.

**Shakespeare's *Much Ado About Nothing*** is a comedy of wit, miscommunication, and the performance of gender. **Denis Villeneuve's *Arrival*** (2016) is a beautifully constructed science fiction film about language, time, and the relationship between how we speak and how we experience the world. It is an inspired choice for close study.

## English Advanced: Textual Conversations

This module pairs texts across time and form to explore how texts speak to and transform each other.

Some notable pairings:

- **Barbara Baynton's *Bush Studies*** (grim, uncompromising short fiction from 1902 about the brutal realities of rural life for women) paired with **Ali Cobby Eckermann's *Inside My Mother*** (poetry rooted in Stolen Generations trauma, country, and Wiradjuri identity). A confronting but rich pairing around land, women, and survival.
- **William Blake's poetry** paired with **Olga Tokarczuk's *Drive Your Plow Over the Bones of the Dead***. Tokarczuk's novel takes its title from a Blake line and builds an eccentric, philosophical murder mystery in rural Poland around a narrator obsessed with Blake and animal rights. The pairing is genuinely unusual and exciting.
- **Keats's odes** paired with **Jane Campion's *Bright Star***, a film about Keats's relationship with Fanny Brawne. The film is lush and attentive to the texture of Keats's world.
- **Shakespeare's *Hamlet*** paired with **Emily Dickinson's *Complete Poems***. Death, consciousness, the interior life: the pairing works on thematic and formal levels.
- **Shakespeare's *Julius Caesar*** paired with **Machiavelli's *The Prince***. Power, political manipulation, and the question of what rulers owe to those they rule.
- **Virginia Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway*** paired with **Stephen Daldry's *The Hours***. The film directly engages with Woolf's novel and her life, making this one of the most explicitly connected pairings on the list.

## English Advanced: Critical Study of Literature

Texts here include **Jane Austen's *Pride and Prejudice***, **Michael Ondaatje's *Warlight*** (a beautifully written novel about memory, wartime London, and the secrets parents keep from their children), and **Shakespeare's *Othello*** and ***King Henry IV, Part 1***.

For poetry: **Louise Glück's selected poems** (including 'The Wild Iris', 'Nostos', 'Vita Nova') bring a Nobel Prize-winning American poet to the HSC for the first time. Glück's work is spare, philosophically serious, and deeply interested in grief, transformation, and the relationship between the human and the natural world. 'The Wild Iris' in particular is extraordinary: poems spoken by flowers, by a gardener, and by God, meditating on consciousness and rebirth.

**W.B. Yeats** needs no introduction. The selected poems cover the full arc of his career: the romanticism of 'The Wild Swans at Coole', the apocalyptic intensity of 'The Second Coming', the late beauty of 'Sailing to Byzantium'.

## A few observations on the list overall

This is a genuinely exciting set of texts. A few things stand out:

- There is a strong commitment to Indigenous Australian voices across both courses and multiple modules: Watson, Cobby Eckermann, Noonuccal, Winch, *One Night the Moon*, *Swallow the Air*.
- Several texts deal directly with language as a theme: Malouf's *An Imaginary Life*, Antrobus's *The Perseverance*, Anderson's *Feed*, Villeneuve's *Arrival*. This is not likely a coincidence.
- The pairing of Blake with Tokarczuk is the most unexpected choice on the list and one of the most intellectually interesting.
- Glück's inclusion in Critical Study is a strong signal that the NESA list is engaging with contemporary international poetry in a serious way.
- Students in Standard have access to some very strong texts. *Swallow the Air*, *Feed*, *Limberlost*, and Antrobus's collection in particular are books that reward rereading and close attention.

The full prescriptions document is available on the NESA website for teachers and students who want the complete detail.
